🎯 Core Finding

The working hypothesis is that the same AI tool can produce different outcomes depending on the user’s cognitive architecture and the work being done. The launch-safe claim is not “AI is better for neurodivergent people.” The stronger, safer claim is: AI value depends on fit, calibration, task, autonomy level, and support boundaries.

📊 Working Pattern

ADHD profile

Potential upside: executive scaffolding, task start, sequencing, working-memory support.

Dyslexia profile

Potential upside: decoding, drafting, formatting, barrier removal.

Autism profile

Potential upside: structure, predictable interaction, communication translation.

Neurotypical profile

Risk: over-reliance, monitoring burden, reduced critical thinking under high automation.
Launch-safe wording: These are observed or hypothesised profile-level patterns. They are not individual prescriptions and must be validated task-by-task.

📈 The Inverted-U Claim

Interpretation: More AI is not automatically better. The useful launch frame is “calibrated assistance,” not “maximum automation.”

⚠️ Risk Analysis

The most important launch message is not the upside. It is the harm boundary: AI can make some people worse at some tasks when intensity is too high or when the system shifts effort from cognition to output-policing.

🔬 Study Design Questions Before Public Release

  • What exactly is a participant: person, response, assessment row, organisation record, or repeated measure?
  • Were cognitive profiles self-reported, screened, clinically diagnosed, or inferred?
  • Which AI tools were used, in what tasks, at what intensity, and how was intensity measured?
  • What is the baseline productivity measure and who scored it?
  • What are the confidence intervals, exclusions, attrition rates, and adverse outcomes?
  • Where are ethics, consent, data governance, and institutional collaboration receipts?
